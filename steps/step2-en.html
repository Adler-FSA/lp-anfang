Step 2 – Centralised or Decentralised? Orientation & Glossary

You now know the basic building blocks (IBAN, exchange, wallet). Here you see where you are in each case: in the world of centralised platforms (CEX) or in the world of decentralised protocols (DEX). You decide whom you trust – a provider or the code.

Mentor note: “If you log in instead of signing, you are in the centralised system.”

Why this step is important

“Centralised” means: you use a service provider that runs accounts, stores data and often also keeps your coins on your behalf. “Decentralised” means: you connect your own wallet directly to a protocol on the blockchain. Both have their place.

What really matters is who holds the keys and how much responsibility you want to carry. This step gives you a clear overview and a compact glossary for the key terms.

Centralised platforms (CEX)

On centralised platforms (Centralised Exchanges, CEX) you sign up with email and password, go through KYC and the provider holds your coins in their system. You see an account balance – but the coins are not directly in your own wallet.

Typical advantages

  • Convenience, app experience, customer support.
  • FIAT deposits (SEPA/card) are often very easy.
  • Wide market coverage (depending on provider many coins/tokens).

Typical disadvantages

  • You hand over data & custody (KYC, central custody).
  • Counterparty and platform risk (the service can have issues).
  • Fees and spreads depend on provider and trading volume.

Examples (not recommendations)

  • Crypto.com – app-based, card/cashback, many coins.
  • Binance – very large selection & liquidity, more complex interface.
  • Coinbase / Bitpanda – more beginner-friendly, usually higher fees.

Note: Always check withdrawal options into your own wallet. “Not your keys, not your coins.”

Decentralised world (DEX & wallets)

In the decentralised world (Decentralised Exchanges, DEX) there is no classic account and no customer database. You connect your wallet directly to a smart contract that executes the swap.

You swap tokens directly for other tokens – peer-to-contract, transparently on-chain. Control stays with you – along with the responsibility.

DEX examples

  • Uniswap (Ethereum) – one of the largest decentralised exchanges.
  • PancakeSwap (BNB Chain) – similar approach, often lower network fees.
  • 1inch / Jupiter / Osmosis – aggregators and DEX in different networks.

Wallet gateway (example: MEW)

  • MEW – MyEtherWallet: non-custodial wallet for Ethereum.
  • Direct wallet connection; swaps via integrated DEX routes.
  • No KYC; you sign with your wallet – full self-responsibility.

Mentor note: “Decentralised does not mean ‘risk-free safety net’. It’s more honest: you are your own support.”

Bridges & hybrids in practice

In real life most people combine centralised and decentralised tools. Typical patterns:

  • CEX purchase → on-chain transfer → DEX swap.
  • FIAT deposit with a centralised service → withdrawal to own wallet → decentralised use.
  • Wallet connect from a CEX app to DEX services (hybrid models).

The lines blur – but the same question remains: Who holds the keys, and where do you carry which responsibility?

Glossary – short & clear Reference

Here you find the most important terms from this step in one place.

Term Meaning
KYC “Know Your Customer” – identity verification required by many centralised services.
CEX Centralised Exchange – trading platform with an operator & account system.
DEX Decentralised Exchange – runs via smart contracts, accessed with your wallet.
Smart contract Program on a blockchain that executes rules automatically once conditions are met.
Gas fee Network fee for transactions (e.g. on Ethereum).
Liquidity pool Pool of tokens that DEX swaps draw from; provided by liquidity providers.
Stablecoin Token pegged to a fiat currency (e.g. USDT, USDC). Designed to be stable, but not risk-free.
Seed phrase 12–24 words that restore your wallet. Never store digitally.
Swap Direct token-to-token exchange, usually on a DEX, without a separate account balance.
Fiat bridge Service that brings bank money into the crypto space (e.g. deposit at an exchange or payment platform).
Non-custodial “Non-custodial” – only you control the keys (e.g. certain wallet apps or hardware wallets).
