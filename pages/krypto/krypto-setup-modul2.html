2 Crypto Setup – Module

Module 2 – Central Setup: Accounts & Exchanges

You build a clear central framework that is tidy, consciously limited and practical for everyday life. After this module you know which accounts and exchanges you actually need, what each one is for and what maximum amounts you are willing to keep there.

This becomes your “ordered playing field” before you later move into decentralized self-custody with your own wallets.

Note: Your entries are stored only locally in your browser (localStorage). You can print this page later with your answers or delete the data for this module again.
Step 1

Mini scenario: “Everything hangs on one account – what then?”

Imagine your main bank account is also the one you use for trading, card payments and all your direct debits. One day the bank freezes it for “security reasons” – maybe just because of a suspicious login. Suddenly rent, bills, salary and your crypto deposits all depend on that single account.

Questions:

  • What chain reaction would that trigger for you today?
  • How much of your financial life currently depends on one account or one exchange?
  • Which entries from your Module 1 inventory are especially “centralized”?

Mini reflection: “Which three spots are currently my biggest single points of failure in the central setup?”

Step 2

Your core structure: 3–4 clear roles instead of account chaos

In Module 2 you move to a simple structure. Not ten new accounts – just clear roles.

2.1 Everyday account

  • Salary, rent, daily living costs.
  • As few experiments as possible.
  • No direct deposits or withdrawals to crypto exchanges.

Which account is (or will be) your pure everyday account?

2.2 Reserve & safety account

  • Cash buffer for 3–6 months of expenses, tax buffers, emergency fund.
  • No constant trading, no impulse emptying.
  • Accessible, but with deliberate friction (separate online banking, maybe a different bank).
Mentor impulse:
“Financial sovereignty doesn’t mean you can empty everything with one tap – it means your safety net doesn’t disappear in a moment of impulse.”

How do you design your reserve & safety account in practice?

2.3 Investment / brokerage account

  • Account for stocks, ETFs and similar investments.
  • Long-term holdings, not your short-term casino.
  • Clearly separated from everyday money and reserves.

Which existing products (ETFs, savings plans, insurance-based investments) clearly belong in the “invest” drawer – and not in “play money”?

2.4 Account / exchange for the crypto on-/off-ramp

  • Bank account and/or central exchange used for fiat ↔ crypto.
  • Later the bridge to your decentralized setup.
  • Chosen consciously – not just “whatever app is advertising hardest”.

Step 3

Safety rules: limits & emergency brake for your central setup

3.1 Maximum balances

You write down upper and lower bounds:

  • “On my everyday account: max. ___ months of expenses.”
  • “On my crypto exchange: max. ___ € equivalent – everything above that will later move to decentralized storage or other layers.”
  • “On my reserve account: at least ___ months of expenses.”
Everyday account – max. … months of expenses
Exchange – maximum asset value in €
Reserve account – at least … months of expenses

3.2 Movement rules

Examples for your own guard rails:

Exchange withdrawals above amount X only after a 24-hour cool-off period.
No direct link: everyday account ↔ exchange (always via a dedicated on-/off-ramp account).
No day trading from the reserve or everyday account.

Your own movement rules (additions):

Mentor impulse:
“You install guard rails before you start driving faster – not after the first crash.”

3.3 Controls & alerts

Concrete steps:

Turn on 2FA on all bank and exchange logins.
Enable e-mail / app notifications for deposits and withdrawals.
Check and use withdrawal whitelists or extra approval steps with your bank / exchange.

Mini task: “Which three security features will I set up or review in the next 7 days?”

Step 4

Link to Module 3: What stays central, what moves to self-custody?

To close the module you prepare the move toward a decentralized setup. You create two columns:

Column A – Stays central

e.g. ETF portfolio, part of your cash reserves, specific insurance products

Column B – Should move to decentralized custody

e.g. long-term crypto positions, emergency reserve in stablecoins, cold storage holdings

Task: “Write down at least three items you want to keep central and at least three you want to move to decentralized custody – with one short sentence why for each.”
