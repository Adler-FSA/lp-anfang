Trading glossary – terms for conscious decisions

This glossary collects key terms around orders, risk, market structure and psychology. All in plain language – so that when you read the trading modules, use the simulator or make real decisions, you always know what the terms actually mean.

Mentor note: Use this glossary as a reference – not to memorise everything, but to make your decisions more conscious, one step at a time.

Area 1

Essentials & market basics

Terms that clarify which playing field you are on.

Spot market
Trading the “real” underlying asset (e.g. stock, coin) without leverage and with direct ownership. Academy focus: for beginners, spot is often the most transparent entry point.
Derivative
Financial product whose value depends on an underlying asset (e.g. CFD, option, future). Important: you are not trading the asset itself, but a contract about it.
Underlying
The asset a derivative is based on – for example a stock, index or coin. If you can’t clearly name the underlying, the product is too fuzzy.
Long
Position that profits from rising prices. Often equated with “investing”, but with leverage a long position can still be highly risky.
Short
Position that profits from falling prices. Shorts are not “evil”, but without clear rules they can produce large losses.
Leverage
Amplifies profits and losses by trading with borrowed capital. In the FSA view, leverage is a tool for advanced users – not a shortcut button.
CFD
“Contract for Difference” – a derivative that lets you trade price differences, often with high leverage. CFDs are easy to access but complex – they belong more in the “later / theory” bucket.
Future
Contract in which two parties agree today on a future trade at a fixed price. Standard instrument for professionals – mainly explained in the FSA context for understanding.
Area 2

Orders & execution

How your trading idea becomes an actual transaction.

Market order
Buy or sell “now at the next available price”. Fast, but with no control over the exact price. Useful in highly liquid markets – risky when liquidity is thin.
Limit order
Order with a price limit: buy at most X, sell at least Y. Helps you avoid chasing prices out of fear of missing out.
Stop order
Order that is only activated once a trigger price is reached (e.g. stop loss). Protection tool – but no substitute for a proper plan.
Stop-limit order
Combination of stop and limit order: trigger first, then place a limit order. Can reduce slippage, but carries the risk of not being filled at all.
Take profit
Predefined price at which profits are automatically realised. Supports you in locking in gains instead of “just a bit more” until they vanish.
Spread
Distance between bid (sell) and ask (buy) price. The wider the spread, the more friction cost – especially relevant for short-term trades.
Slippage
Difference between the expected and the actual execution price. Common with market orders during high volatility or low liquidity.
Order book
Overview of current buy and sell orders in a market. For advanced traders a way to see where liquidity is concentrated.
Area 3

Risk & money management

Terms around position size, losses and protection.

Account size
Total value of your trading account, including open positions and free cash. All percentage risk figures are based on this number.
Position size
Volume of a single position (e.g. number of shares or contracts). Connects your stop distance with the money amount you put at risk.
Risk per trade (R)
Amount you are willing to lose at most in one trade. For beginners, 0.5–1% of account size per trade is often a sensible upper bound.
Risk–reward ratio (RRR)
Ratio between potential profit and planned risk. An RRR of 3:1 means you risk 1 unit to aim for 3 units of profit.
Drawdown
Decline of your account from the last equity high to the current level. How you handle drawdowns tells more about your robustness than your best winning streak.
Margin
Collateral you must provide when trading leveraged products. If losses grow too large, the remaining margin may no longer be sufficient.
Margin call
Broker’s request to add funds or close positions because your margin is too low. Usually a symptom that risk was not controlled early enough.
Over-leveraging
Combination of high leverage, large position size and tight stops. Looks small on paper, but in practice it can cause fast and deep drawdowns.
Area 4

Market behaviour & trading styles

How markets typically move – and how you place yourself in time.

Volatility
Measure of how widely a market moves up and down. High volatility means more movement, not automatically more opportunity.
Trend
Dominant direction of a market (up, down or sideways). Many strategies only work when you respect the higher timeframe trend.
Range
Sideways phase in which price oscillates between support and resistance. Trend-following approaches often struggle in ranges – and vice versa.
Timeframe
Time unit of the chart you look at (e.g. 5 minutes, 1 hour, daily). Your timeframe should fit your life, not your ego.
Day trading
Opening and closing positions within the same trading day. Feels active and exciting, but demands a strict rule set and a lot of focus.
Swing trading
Holding positions for several days or weeks to capture larger moves. For many people with jobs, often more realistic than pure day trading.
Scalping
Very short-term trading with many small trades and minutes of holding time. Not suitable for beginners – costs, stress and mistakes add up quickly.
Liquidity
How easily large orders can be executed without moving the price much. Good liquidity reduces slippage and spread – an important quality factor.
Area 5

Psychology & behaviour

Why many problems don’t live in the chart, but in our head.

FOMO (fear of missing out)
Fear of missing a chance – typical trigger for late, impulsive entries. In the FSA approach, high FOMO is a sign to reduce size, not to speed up.
Overtrading
Taking too many trades in too little time – often out of boredom or pressure. Signals that your plan lacks clear conditions and built-in breaks.
Revenge trading
Trying to “win back” losses quickly with new trades. Danger zone – the right move here is pause, review, reset.
Confirmation bias
Tendency to seek information that confirms our view and ignore what contradicts it. In trading this often leads to ignoring stops and red flags.
Loss aversion
Losses feel psychologically stronger than gains of the same size. That’s why many traders hold losers too long and take winners too early.
Plan discipline
Ability to execute your rules even when it feels uncomfortable. Within the Academy, this is a key success factor – more important than any single setup.
Break-even mindset
Desire to at least exit a losing trade at no loss. Can be useful – but becomes dangerous when you move stops just to avoid taking a small, planned loss.
Mentor space
Dedicated space in which you review trades calmly (journal, simulator, discussions). This is where the FSA Academy connects: reflection instead of just “next trade”.
